## The Science of Luck Perception and Decision-Making

Luck isn't just chance; it's a lens through which we view the world. Psychologists like Richard Wiseman, author of *The Luck Factor*, have spent decades studying why some folks attract good fortune while others repel it. The key? **Luck perception**. People who see themselves as lucky are more open to opportunities, take calculated risks, and bounce back from setbacks faster.

Consider this: in one famous experiment, Wiseman gave participants a newspaper and asked them to count the photos. "Lucky" people spotted a bold message on page 2 saying, "Stop counting—there are 43 photos." Unlucky folks missed it entirely, too wrapped up in anxiety or tunnel vision. This highlights how **luck perception** influences decision-making. Optimists notice serendipity; pessimists fixate on obstacles.

Neuroscience backs this up. Brain scans show that positive outlooks activate the prefrontal cortex, enhancing creativity and problem-solving. A study from University College London found that those who believe in luck make 20% better financial decisions because they're less paralyzed by fear. Flip side: chronic "bad luck" believers suffer higher stress hormones, clouding judgment.
